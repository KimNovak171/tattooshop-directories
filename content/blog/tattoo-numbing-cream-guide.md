Topical numbing creams have become more widely used in tattooing over the past decade, and there is genuine debate within the tattooing community about their use. Understanding what these products actually do, when they are appropriate, and how to use them correctly helps you make an informed decision.

## How Tattoo Numbing Creams Work

Topical numbing products used for tattooing typically contain lidocaine, a local anesthetic that temporarily blocks nerve signal transmission in the area where it is applied. When applied correctly to clean skin and left in place for the appropriate time, lidocaine reduces the pain sensation in the treated area.

The effectiveness of numbing creams varies considerably based on the specific product, how correctly it is applied, the individual's skin and how well the cream absorbs, and the location being treated. In general, numbing creams reduce pain rather than eliminate it completely, and their effectiveness diminishes over the duration of a long session as the anesthetic wears off.

## Common Products

Several numbing products are widely used in the tattoo industry. EMLA cream is a pharmaceutical product available by prescription in some countries and over the counter in others, containing both lidocaine and prilocaine. Hush and other tattoo-specific numbing products are available from tattoo supply retailers and online. Dr. Numb and similar consumer products are widely available.

Products vary in their concentration of active anesthetic and in their formulation, which affects both effectiveness and how they interact with the skin during tattooing.

## The Artist's Perspective

Many tattoo artists have reservations about numbing creams, and these reservations are worth understanding. Some numbing products alter the texture of the skin in a way that the artist finds affects how the needle moves through the skin and how ink is deposited. A skin that feels different than normal can make precise technical work more difficult.

Some artists are entirely comfortable with clients using numbing products and have adapted their technique accordingly. Others prefer not to work with them or have specific product preferences. Always discuss numbing cream use with your artist before your appointment rather than applying it without mentioning it — this is a professional courtesy that ensures everyone is on the same page.

## When Numbing Products Make Sense

For placements that are consistently rated as high-pain areas — ribs, sternum, hands, feet, spine — numbing cream can make the experience significantly more tolerable and allow for longer sessions without breaks. For clients with low pain tolerance or significant anxiety about pain, reducing that barrier can make the difference between getting a tattoo they have wanted for years and continuing to postpone.

For very long sessions, numbing can reduce fatigue and allow the client to hold position more comfortably, which benefits the quality of the work.

## How to Apply Correctly

If you plan to use a numbing cream, apply it to clean, dry skin at least forty-five minutes to an hour before your appointment, following the product's specific instructions. Apply a generous but not excessive layer and cover it with plastic wrap to keep it in contact with the skin and prevent evaporation.

Remove the wrap and any residue just before the tattoo begins. Inform your artist that you have applied numbing cream.

## Managing Expectations

Numbing cream reduces pain but does not eliminate it entirely. Deep linework and areas where the numbing has begun to wear off during a long session will still be felt. Going in with the expectation of significant reduction rather than complete absence of sensation produces more realistic outcomes. Many clients find that even partial numbing makes a significant difference in their overall experience, particularly in sensitive areas.
