Tattooing involves breaking the skin, and any time the skin is broken, the risk of infection and bloodborne pathogen transmission exists if proper sanitation protocols are not followed. Understanding what proper standards look like empowers you to make informed choices about where you get tattooed.

## Licensing and Regulation

In most US states and Canadian provinces, tattoo shops are required to be licensed and are subject to health inspections. A shop's license should be prominently displayed in the shop. Ask to see it if it is not visible. Artists themselves are also required to hold individual licenses in many jurisdictions, and these should also be available for inspection.

Health department inspection records are public records in most jurisdictions. Some states post inspection reports online. Checking inspection history before choosing a shop is a worthwhile step for clients who prioritize safety.

## Autoclave Sterilization

The autoclave is the standard piece of equipment used to sterilize reusable equipment — metal tubes, grips, and other hardware that is used across multiple clients. It uses pressurized steam at high temperature to kill all microorganisms including bacterial spores.

Autoclaves should be present and visibly maintained in the shop. Ask when it was last tested. Autoclaves require regular spore testing to verify they are functioning correctly — a responsible shop tests its autoclave regularly and keeps documentation of those tests.

## Single-Use Needles

Tattoo needles must be single-use and should be opened in a sealed sterile package in front of you at the start of your appointment. Never accept a tattoo from an artist who cannot demonstrate that they are opening fresh needles for your service.

The same applies to ink caps. Ink should be poured into individual disposable caps for each client rather than used directly from shared bottles. Dipping a contaminated needle back into a shared ink bottle can transmit pathogens to subsequent clients.

## Gloves and Cross-Contamination

Artists should wash their hands and put on fresh gloves before beginning any work on a client. They should change gloves during the session if they touch something that could contaminate the gloves — a phone, a bottle cap, their own face. 

Cross-contamination is one of the more subtle safety issues in tattooing. Watch how your artist handles items during your session. If they touch their phone while tattooed and then continue without changing gloves, that is a contamination event that a safety-conscious artist would address.

## Surface Preparation and Barrier Protection

The surfaces of the tattoo station — the chair or table, the arm rest, the bottle surfaces — should be covered with disposable barrier protection or thoroughly cleaned with an EPA-registered disinfectant between clients. This prevents cross-contamination between clients who use the same equipment.

## Red Flags to Watch For

Several warning signs indicate inadequate sanitation standards. A shop that looks generally dirty or disorganized, with surfaces that appear not to have been cleaned, is a basic concern. An artist who does not wash their hands before beginning, who opens needles without showing you the sealed packaging, or who uses ink directly from shared bottles without pouring into caps is not following proper protocols.

An artist who becomes defensive or dismissive when you ask about their sanitation practices is telling you something important. A shop that takes sanitation seriously will welcome these questions.

## Your Right to Ask Questions

You have every right to ask any safety question before allowing anyone to tattoo you. Asking where needles come from, how equipment is sterilized, and how surfaces are cleaned between clients is not paranoid or rude — it is informed and responsible behavior that any reputable shop will treat with respect.

If you feel uncomfortable with any aspect of a shop's sanitation practices, trust that instinct and take your business elsewhere. Your health is not worth compromising for convenience or cost savings.

The most satisfying tattoo experiences consistently come from preparation, honest communication, and genuine trust in a skilled artist. Every step you take before sitting in the chair — researching your artist, clarifying your vision, preparing your body and mind for the session — contributes directly to the quality of the result you carry for the rest of your life. Tattooing is one of the oldest forms of personal artistic expression, and approaching it with the care and intentionality it deserves produces work that genuinely reflects who you are and what you value. Working with an artist you have researched thoroughly, communicating your vision clearly, and following professional guidance on design and placement are the three habits that most reliably produce tattoos that look beautiful, heal well, and continue to feel meaningful for decades after the appointment.
